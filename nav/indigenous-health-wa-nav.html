Health Coverage for Tribal Members
in Washington State

For enrolled members of federally recognized tribes in Washington State · Version 1 · May 2026

How I found this pathway — and why it took so long →
The short version

If you are an enrolled member of a federally recognized tribe, there is a pathway to a private health insurance plan through Washington State's marketplace that can give you full coverage with no deductible, no copays, and no coinsurance — and you may be able to get it any month of the year, not just during open enrollment.

Most people don't know this exists. This guide walks you through what the benefit is, who qualifies, what you need, and how to actually get it in Washington State.

The Affordable Care Act includes special protections for American Indian and Alaska Native people. One of those protections is that if your household income is at or below 300% of the federal poverty level, you can enroll in a marketplace plan and pay nothing at the point of care — no deductible, no copay, no coinsurance — on any plan tier. You still pay a monthly premium, but premium subsidies are available that can significantly reduce or sometimes eliminate that cost.

This is different from Apple Health (Medicaid). It is private insurance accessed through Washington's marketplace, with the federal trust responsibility embedded in how your costs are structured.

Eligibility

Who this applies to

You qualify for these special AI/AN marketplace protections if you are:

  • An enrolled member of a federally recognized tribe, or
  • A shareholder in an Alaska Native Claims Settlement Act (ANCSA) Corporation

Your family members who are not tribal members or ANCSA shareholders do not qualify for the same zero cost-sharing benefit, even if you apply together. More on that below under Mixed-Status Households.

You do not have to live on a reservation. You do not have to currently use Indian Health Service. You just have to be an enrolled member of a federally recognized tribe and be able to document it.

Income limit

The income threshold

The zero cost-sharing benefit applies if your household income is at or below 300% of the federal poverty level. For 2026 coverage, the relevant numbers (based on 2025 federal poverty guidelines) are roughly:

Household size 300% FPL (annual) 300% FPL (monthly)
1 person$45,180$3,765
2 people$61,320$5,110
3 people$79,950$6,663
4+ peopleCheck Washington Healthplanfinder directly — threshold increases with each additional person

Note: some income from tribal trust land, treaty rights, and federal trust payments may not count toward this limit. Per capita gaming income does count. See What Income Counts? below.

If your income is above 300% FPL, you still qualify for marketplace plans and may still qualify for premium subsidies, but the zero cost-sharing benefit at point of care no longer applies.

The benefit

What "zero cost-sharing" actually means

When you have this benefit and you see an in-network provider, you pay nothing out of pocket for covered services beyond your monthly premium. That means:

  • No deductible to meet before coverage kicks in
  • No copays at appointments
  • No coinsurance (the percentage you'd normally pay after a deductible)

This applies to any metal-tier plan (Bronze, Silver, Gold, Platinum). The cost-sharing protection is the same regardless of which tier you choose.

There is one important exception: if you get care from an Indian Health Service facility, tribal clinic, or urban Indian health program, you have zero out-of-pocket costs regardless of your income. That is a separate protection that exists even outside this marketplace benefit.

Enrollment

You can enroll any month

Most people have to wait for the annual open enrollment window (November through mid-January). Tribal members and ANCSA shareholders can enroll in or change a marketplace plan any month of the year through a monthly special enrollment period.

This is significant. If your income changes, your situation changes, or you just found out this benefit exists, you do not have to wait.

Apple Health vs. marketplace

What about Apple Health (Medicaid)?

In Washington State, if your household income is below 138% of the federal poverty level, you will likely be directed to Apple Health rather than a marketplace plan. Apple Health is free and covers most services, and AI/AN people have additional Apple Health protections including no cost-sharing for services at IHS, tribal, and urban Indian facilities.

If you are above 138% FPL and below 300% FPL, the marketplace zero cost-sharing pathway is often the better option because it gives you access to a broader provider network than Apple Health while still costing you nothing at the point of care.

If you are right at or near 138% FPL, it is worth comparing what you would actually get through each program before choosing.

Monthly cost

What about premiums?

You still pay a monthly premium for the marketplace plan. Premium tax credits (subsidies) are available to help reduce that cost.

Important for 2026: The enhanced premium subsidies that were in place from 2021 through 2025 expired at the end of 2025 and were not renewed by Congress. Premiums are higher than last year for many people, and subsidies no longer cover people above 400% FPL. If you were enrolled before and your premium changed significantly, this is why.

Washington State also has its own additional premium subsidy program (Cascade Care Savings) that can help lower costs further for people who qualify. This is separate from the federal premium tax credit and is specific to Washington.

The best way to see your actual premium after subsidies is to run through Washington Healthplanfinder with your actual income numbers. The estimated cost you see there reflects both federal and state subsidies.

IHS and tribal clinics

What about using IHS and tribal clinics?

Enrolling in a marketplace plan does not give up your access to Indian Health Service, tribal health programs, or urban Indian health programs. You can continue to use those services the same way you do now. Having a marketplace plan actually expands your options — your tribal clinic stays available, and you also gain access to the broader network of providers in your plan.

When you use your marketplace plan at a non-IHS provider, the plan becomes the primary payer, and IHS or tribal funding is secondary. This is how it is supposed to work — it draws down more resources into your care.

Income calculation

What income counts?

Income calculations for marketplace plans use a measure called Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI). For AI/AN applicants, some income that would normally count is excluded:

  • Income from Indian trust land (rents, royalties, use rights) generally does not count
  • Income from treaty fishing rights and similar treaty-protected activities generally does not count
  • Per capita income from tribal gaming generally does count and must be reported

If you have income from tribal sources and you are unsure whether it counts, Washington Healthplanfinder can walk through it with you, or you can request help from a Navigator or Certified Application Counselor.

Mixed households

Mixed-status households

If your household includes both tribal members and non-members, the zero cost-sharing benefit only applies to the tribal members. You can apply on the same Healthplanfinder application for the purpose of getting premium subsidies for everyone, but the tribal members should enroll in a separate plan from the non-members to actually access the zero cost-sharing benefit.

If you are all on the same plan, the cost-sharing protection for the tribal members may not apply correctly. This is one of the most common navigation mistakes. A tribal assister or Navigator can make sure you are structured correctly before you finalize your enrollment.

Documentation

What you need to apply

  • Proof of tribal enrollment — any official document issued by your tribe that shows you are an enrolled member. This could be your tribal ID card, a letter from enrollment, or another document that identifies you as a member. You have 90 days from application to provide documentation.
  • Income information — your best estimate of your 2026 household income
  • Social Security numbers for all household members applying
  • Basic household information — size, ages
Where to apply

Where to go in Washington State

Washington uses its own marketplace, called Washington Healthplanfinder. Go to wahealthplanfinder.org to apply. Do not use healthcare.gov — that is the federal marketplace for states that do not have their own.

When you get to the application, mark that you are a tribal member. This triggers the AI/AN-specific questions and unlocks the zero cost-sharing option if you are eligible.

Free help is available from:

Navigators and Certified Application Counselors

Trained assistants who can walk through the application with you at no cost. Find them through wahealthplanfinder.org or by calling 1-855-923-4633.

Your tribal health department or benefits coordinator

Many tribes have staff who can help members navigate the marketplace application and understand their options. Tribal assisters are trained specifically on AI/AN marketplace rules.

Urban Indian health programs

If you live off-reservation in an urban area, your local urban Indian health program may have enrollment support.

Common problems

Things that can go wrong

The marketplace puts you in Apple Health instead of a marketplace plan.

If your income is entered a certain way, the system may redirect you to Apple Health. This can be the right call depending on your situation, but if you want to use the marketplace zero cost-sharing pathway, make sure your income is entered accurately and that your tribal status is marked. A Navigator can help you if the system is not giving you the option you expect.

You end up on a plan with your non-tribal family members.

As noted above, this means the zero cost-sharing protection may not apply to you. Separate plans for tribal and non-tribal household members is the right structure.

You do not know which plan to pick.

With zero cost-sharing, any metal tier covers your costs at the point of care. The main variable is your monthly premium after subsidies. Run the comparison in Healthplanfinder and look at what your actual monthly cost would be for each plan. A Bronze plan typically has the lowest premium; with subsidies, a Silver plan is sometimes cheaper than it appears.

Your income changes mid-year.

You can report income changes to Healthplanfinder and adjust your coverage. This matters because your premium subsidy is based on your estimated annual income. If your income goes up significantly, you may owe back some of the subsidy at tax time. Report changes when they happen to avoid a surprise.

Why this exists

The bigger context

This benefit exists because the federal government has a trust responsibility to tribal nations — a legal and political obligation that flows from treaty relationships and the sovereign-to-sovereign relationship between tribes and the United States. The zero cost-sharing protections in the ACA are one expression of that responsibility in the context of health care. IHS and tribal health programs are another.

These protections are not charity. They are not a government favor. They are part of what the federal government owes to tribal nations as part of that foundational relationship.

Knowing they exist, and knowing how to use them, is part of how that relationship becomes real rather than just words in a law.
